What has added your interest to learn some computer programming?

I was pretty much into computer games than all of those other childhood activities, whether it was drawing stuff on the driveway or road with chalk, playing baseball or tennis, biking around the block, or having fun at the playground. The real culprit came from when I participated in a summer camp at McGill University in 2010 called "Be A Computer Scientist for A Week." Even though I took it so seriously and really was so hard on myself I got stressed out at certain points, it made me earn a passion for this that was built through my college education.

What's your passion that impacts what you want to do in life?

Building things where the design is more from myself than just having to stick to the design by something else. One habit I had when I was so young is drawing so many things out on paper to replicate sequels of video games; even though I drew mostly user interfaces and not the game environments, I was kind of creative.
I even took some art courses in high school where even though it was kind of hard to understand some things that my teachers were looking for, I would try to design things that looked so catchy beyond the mind's eye, making sure I kept myself up to what the teachers expected so that I would not break the rules. One of my favourite art assignments was doing this promotion poster of a variety show at my high school to celebrate the end of a school year, where it did took longer than what my teacher expected, but I ended up finishing it anyway because I just wanted to see it finished.

Why Software Engineering and not Computer Science?

From not doing a lot of research back in 2014, I think Software Engineering simply involves the application of engineering practices to software development. Even though some of the theories I've learned in CS were pretty interesting at the time, I feel that it was more valuable for me to take on the side to which I would want to build something creative instead of consistently performing research and identifying what it is we can expand upon in the electronics side.
Even though technically at UWaterloo, Software Engineering is a combination of Electrical and Computer Engineering, and Computer Science, there are so many realities I would learn along the way that I can apply in my responsibilities when I am in my career. It's simply applying critical theories while building on this sort of immense amount of code so that customers can have a surprising first reaction and that certain things would be seen as innovative or just some casual practice.

Software engineering itself is a very broad discipline. What sub-disciplines do you like the most?

For this, I am taking the theme of this as discussed from a page offered by the Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.
It would be software development, because that's where I can apply both my coding knowledge and possibly some mathematics or physics. (There are many applications alone where mathematics or physics really isn't involved, but since I went through both single variable calculus and multivariate calculus, I would want to try to program those in. Otherwise, the majority of it is just memory management, I think.) That's where I would put in my passion for building something, because that final project involves a whole bunch of components that are supposed to work together so that it turns out to be something useful.
I am not practically sure about software design, because the final code is often totally different than what's theorized. Even if I were to use some psuedocode and try out different ways to go about solving a problem, it takes a long time to analyze those theoretical algorithms to make sure they work in different circumstances. This is more for safety critical systems, I think, but there are applications of this to building something as well.
I still somewhat like it, because if I were to consider a software project alone, I need to see how often it is useful for a purpose, or how compatible it would be if I was to build it to different systems such as a Mac or a PlayStation 4.

Do you feel that engineering is really what you belong to? You know, engineering is really you?

Of course! In addition, since the University of Waterloo is more of an engineering institution, I got this vast network of students to chat with to talk about things I want to build and discover some stuff that I would find interesting or eventually useful for what I want to build.

What would be the symbols you see in recognition of UWaterloo's pride heritage of engineering?

Obviously, our faculty symbol is "The Tool", this pipe wrench that's five feet long and forged by Ridgid. However, we run a student society within the faculty, where the logo is actually comprising of four parts with each one representing a major engineering discipline: a hollow 12-tooth spur gear cut in half (Mechanical engineering), this lightning bolt (Electrical engineering), a test tube with a drip coming out (Chemical engineering), and a transit (Civil engineering).

Do you earn some hardhats for being an engineering student at the University of Waterloo?

You bet! They are granted to you when you go through the UWaterloo Engineering Orientation Week, which is supposed to spread some awareness to newcomer students about the different facilities and services offered at UWaterloo. These hardhats serve more than just having to protect yourself; they symbolize dedicated hard work to projects, potentially to some leadership and pride.
You start out with a yellow hardhat. If you decide later to volunteer for that orientation week, you'll get a different colored hardhat that not only represents your ranking within the Orientation Week, but also your dedication.
A green hardhat would represent you're going forward with the committment to leadership and that you're starting to learn and practice some fundamental engineering skills. A red one shows a more experienced style of leadership, with the ability to recall past previous knowledge to apply it in more complicated tasks in a general sense, symbolizing potential influence with elegant communication. The toughness really comes in when you happen to have a black hardhat; that's where you need to teach those newcomers with very heavy discipline, yet in a way that lets them think UWaterloo is fun in general.
Of course, the actual highest hardhat you can go for, if you are committed to helping everyone out with the proper amount of leadership training, communication, and efficiency at both homework and volunteering, is believe it or not, a white hardhat with so many details on it. It represents absolute prestige and caring.
There are requirements to get each one, and it is sort of like a totem pole where you have to start from the bottom and these helmets come one after another. But I'm not going to explain the details.
